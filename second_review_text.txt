EXISTING SYSTEM
1.
Rule-Based Chatbots(Examples:
Woebot
,
Wysa
)
Rule-based chatbots like
Woebot
and
Wysa
operate on simple keyword matching. When a user types a message, the bot identifies a keyword and returns a pre-written response — the same answer every time, regardless of context or emotional depth. There is no real AI understanding behind the conversation. After just 2 to 3 sessions, patients begin to feel the robotic nature of the interaction. These systems have zero clinical diagnosis ability, carry no memory between sessions, and involve no doctor at any point in the process.
2.LLM Wrappers(Examples: ChatGPT-based Mental Health Bots)
LLM-based mental health bots are essentially ChatGPT with a mental health prompt layered on top. The user's message — including all personal details — is sent directly to OpenAI's servers with no anonymization or privacy filtering. These systems have no emotion classification, no PHQ or MHQ scoring, and no follow-up mechanism. Because they are generative models, they carry a high hallucination risk — meaning they can confidently produce incorrect or even harmful clinical advice. They are not HIPAA compliant and have no awareness of the patient's history or mental health trajectory.
3.Traditional Therapy Apps(Examples:
BetterHelp
,
Talkspace
)
Traditional therapy platforms like
BetterHelp
and
Talkspace
connect patients with licensed human therapists. While this provides genuine clinical value, it comes with serious limitations in scalability and accessibility. Sessions cost between $60 to $100 per hour, require patients to wait days or even weeks for an appointment, and offer no real-time monitoring between sessions. There is no crisis detection between appointments, no automated intervention if a patient stops engaging, and no 24/7 availability. For patients in rural India — where language barriers and cultural stigma already limit access — these platforms are practically out of reach.

Core Architecture – n8n Automation Engine
Entry Point —
FastAPI
Webhook
This is where everything begins. A patient sends a message (likely through a chat interface or WhatsApp), and that message hits your
FastAPI
webhook
as an HTTP POST request.
FastAPI
acts as the front door — it receives the raw message payload and immediately passes it into the n8n workflow. This is your system's trigger node, meaning nothing runs until this webhook fires.
Branch 1 — Premium Pipeline (The Core AI Chain)
When the Triage node classifies a message as premium, it enters a
sequential 3-AI chain
— each model building on the previous one's output:
Step 1 — Gemini (Diagnosis Engine)
The patient's message is sent to
Google's Gemini
via the Generative Language API. Gemini's job is purely clinical — it analyses the symptoms, duration, and severity described by the patient and generates a
structured clinical diagnosis
. It might return a differential diagnosis list or a primary suspected condition. This output is not patient-facing yet — it's raw clinical data.
--- Slide 21 ---
Step 2 — Claude (Empathy Story Engine)
Gemini's clinical output is then passed to
Anthropic's Claude
. Claude's job is to take that cold, clinical diagnosis and
wrap it in compassionate, human language
. It rewrites the information so the patient doesn't feel like they're reading a medical report. Claude handles the emotional layer — reducing anxiety, using culturally sensitive language, and making the patient feel heard. This is why Claude specifically was chosen for this step — it excels at nuanced, empathetic communication.
Step 3 — ChatGPT (Options Engine)
Claude's empathetic response is then passed to
OpenAI's ChatGPT
. ChatGPT's role is to generate
clear, actionable next steps
for the patient — things like "visit a clinic," "take these OTC medications," "call emergency services," or "monitor at home." It formats the response as a numbered menu of options so the patient knows exactly what to do next. This is the final response that gets sent back to the patient.
Branch 2 — Normal Chat
If the Triage node decides the message is a simple, non-clinical query, it skips all three AI models entirely and
returns a standard conversational reply
directly. This keeps costs low and response time fast for messages that don't need clinical processing — things like greetings, appointment questions, or general information requests.
--- Slide 22 ---
Branch 3 — Crisis Protocol (Safety-Critical Path)
This is arguably the most important branch from a
patient safety
perspective. When the Triage node detects crisis indicators — suicidal ideation, self-harm language, medical emergencies — two things happen simultaneously or in sequence:
WhatsApp SOS Alert (Twilio)
An automated WhatsApp message is instantly fired to a
designated emergency contact
— likely a clinical supervisor, on-call doctor, or crisis team — via the Twilio API. This happens without any human initiating it. The clinician is alerted in real time.
Return Crisis Protocol
A
pre-written, clinically-approved crisis response
is sent back to the patient. This is hardcoded content — it does NOT go through Gemini, Claude, or ChatGPT. This is a deliberate safety design decision: LLMs can hallucinate, and a hallucinated response to someone in crisis could be catastrophic. The response contains things like helpline numbers, grounding instructions, and immediate next steps.

CONCLUSION
Keffi AI is not just a traditional chatbot but a clinically inspired digital mental health platform that combines artificial intelligence with proven psychological frameworks to deliver personalized and real-time support. By understanding 96+ emotional and depression states, integrating therapeutic response strategies like CBT and empathy-based interaction, and enabling automation through systems like n8n, the platform ensures both intelligent conversation and timely intervention. future integration with wearable devices, Keffi AI moves towards a proactive and continuous mental healthcare
model. Overall, it represents a shift from reactive chatbot systems to a smart, scalable, and clinically aware mental
health ecosystem capable of supporting users effectively and bridging the gap between technology and healthcare.
